## Part I — What Is an Adjudicated Program? — 8 min

### The Colorado Landscape

Three primary organizations run adjudicated large ensemble events in Colorado:

**CBA (Colorado Bandmasters Association) — Concert Band Festival**
- State Concert Band Festival is the flagship event for concert bands
- Ensembles perform a prepared program and receive recorded commentary from adjudicators plus an individualized clinic
- Rating scale: Superior (I), Excellent (II), Good (III), Fair (IV), Poor (V); "Superior with Distinction" is the top designation
- CBA maintains a Prescribed Music List (PML) — the database tracks this in the `On CBA PML` column
- Festivals run at regional sites (including Grand Junction) before state-level qualification
- Non-qualifying festivals exist for developing bands and second/third ensembles

**Colorado ASTA — Large Group Orchestra Festival**
- Regional festivals in early March; State Festival in late April
- 30-minute warm-up → 30-minute performance window (20 minutes of music maximum) → 30-minute clinic with a national-level clinician
- No prescribed list — music selection is entirely the director's choice
- Must provide 4 score copies per piece for adjudicators
- If selected for State, you must add or change one piece
- Required to listen to two other performing groups at the festival

**CMEA (Colorado Music Educators Association)**
- Hosts Solo & Ensemble Festivals and supports large ensemble events
- Annual Clinic/Conference brings together Colorado music educators
- Art music (not pop music) must be performed; adjudicators may lower ratings for non-art music
- Sight-reading may be optional, but opting out disqualifies from top rating designation

### The Universal Anatomy of an Adjudicated Program

Regardless of the specific organization, a well-constructed adjudicated program typically includes:

1. **A warm-up selection** — sets tone, gets the ensemble's sound established in the hall
2. **Two to three contrasting prepared selections** — this is the meat; pieces should differ in style, tempo, key, and character
3. **Sight-reading** (where required or elected) — demonstrates the ensemble's musicianship beyond prepared repertoire

Key concept: **Adjudicators evaluate against the score, not against other ensembles.** This is assessment, not competition. They're asking: *Did this ensemble realize what the composer wrote? Did the director select music appropriate to the ensemble's level?*

### What "Contrasting" Actually Means

A strong program demonstrates range across multiple dimensions (connect to SHMRG if students have that framework):

- **Tempo:** fast/slow
- **Style/character:** lyrical vs. rhythmic, legato vs. marcato
- **Historical period/genre:** don't put three 21st-century American works on the same program
- **Tonality:** vary keys, modes, harmonic language
- **Form:** show your students can handle different structural designs

**The rule of thumb:** If two pieces on the same program are too similar in most of those categories, one of them probably doesn't belong.

## Part II — Meet the Database — 10 min

### Why This Tool Exists

*"There are thousands of pieces out there. The question isn't 'what exists?' — it's 'what's been vetted, by whom, and how well does it hold up under real-world conditions?'"*

These databases aggregate real performance data and expert curation into a single searchable resource. They answer the question every new teacher has: *Where do I start?*

### The Band Database (FL MPA Repertoire Database)

**The basics:** 19,508 band originals + 282 transcriptions/arrangements. Aggregates 37,580 Florida MPA performances (2009–2020), cross-referenced with 22 state prescribed lists and multiple professional reference sources.

**The columns that matter most:**

| Column | What It Tells You |
|--------|-------------------|
| **Best Bet (0–100)** | The single best column to sort. Composite score = 60% MPA Confidence + 40% Normalized Street Cred. |
| **MPA Confidence (0–100)** | How reliably this piece earns strong adjudication scores, normalized for grade level. |
| **Street Cred (0–15.5)** | How widely vetted this piece is across professional sources (Krienes, TMTP, BDG, CBA PML, state lists, ICD, Director Tiers). Additive scoring. |
| **Grade (1–6)** | Difficulty level. Your program should include a spread — not all at grade 5. |
| **Raw MPA Score (0–100)** | Wilson Score confidence interval before grade normalization. |
| **Trend Direction** | Is this piece's adjudication performance improving, stable, or declining over time? |

**Street Cred breakdown — where the points come from:**

- Krienes Concert Repertoire Guide: +3 (stackable if on both editions)
- TMTP (Teaching Music Through Performance): +3
- Director Tier 1 (Bimm/Snoeck taught it 2+ times): +3
- BDG (Band Directors Guide) Common: +2
- ICD URM (underrepresented composer): +2
- CBA PML: +1
- State list presence: +0.01 per state (up to 22 states)
- Director Tier 2: +0.3
- BDG Proposed: +0.5

**Other useful columns:**

- `On CBA PML` — directly relevant if programming for a CBA festival
- `State List Count` — how many states include this on their prescribed list (max 22)
- `Categories` — genre/style tags from WindRep.org (folksong-based, marches, award winners, etc.)
- `Style Tags` — more granular descriptors (lyrical, intonation_sensitive, mixed_meter, etc.)
- `ICD Diversity / ICD Detail` — flags underrepresented composers with demographic categories

### The Orchestra Database

**The basics:** 3,990 entries cross-referenced from TMTP Orchestra (Vols 1–4), SCSBOA (California), PMEA (Pennsylvania), and the Institute for Composer Diversity.

**Key difference from the band database:** No MPA adjudication data. The scoring is entirely source-based.

| Column | What It Tells You |
|--------|-------------------|
| **Best Bet (0–100)** | Normalized Street Cred. Best single column to sort. |
| **Street Cred (0–7)** | TMTP +3, ICD URM +2, SCSBOA +1, PMEA +1. |
| **Ensemble** | String, Full, or both. Crucial for knowing if your ensemble can actually play it. |
| **Grade (0.5–6)** | Grade levels vary between sources; SCSBOA uses 0.5–5, PMEA and TMTP use 1–6. |
| **On TMTP / TMTP Grade / TMTP Vol** | Presence in Teaching Music Through Performance in Orchestra. |

### Reading the Data — What to Notice

Walk through a concrete example: **Shenandoah (Ticheli)**

- Best Bet: 97.42 — near the top of the entire database
- MPA Confidence: 95.7 — adjudicators consistently rate this highly
- Street Cred: 15.48 — the maximum practical score; it's on everything
- Grade 4 — accessible to strong MS or good HS band
- 140 total performances across 103 unique schools
- On 18 state lists, on Krienes, on TMTP, on CBA PML, on BDG
- Trend: Stable — it's been reliable for a decade
- ICD: not flagged (Ticheli is not an underrepresented composer)

This is the kind of piece that anchors a program. Now: what goes *next to it*?

## Part III — Group Activity: Build a Mock Program — 12 min

### Setup (2 min)

Split into groups of 3–4. Each group gets a scenario:

**Scenario cards (examples):**
1. You direct a **Grade 3 middle school band** entering a CBA regional concert festival. Build a 3-piece adjudicated program.
2. You direct a **Grade 5 high school wind ensemble** preparing for the CBA State Concert Band Festival. Build a 3-piece program.
3. You direct a **Grade 3 high school string orchestra** preparing for a Colorado ASTA Large Group Festival. Build a program (20 min max).
4. You direct a **Grade 2 middle school full orchestra** preparing for a Colorado ASTA regional festival. Build a program.

### The Task (8 min)

Using the database (either projected or on shared screen/printout):

1. **Filter to your grade level**
2. **Sort by Best Bet** (highest first)
3. **Select 2–3 pieces that create a contrasting program** — defend your choices using the data
4. Be ready to explain:
   - Why each piece? (What does the data tell you?)
   - How do the pieces contrast with each other?
   - What does this program teach the students?
   - Is at least one piece by an underrepresented composer? (Check ICD columns)

### Debrief (2 min)

Each group shares their top pick and one sentence about why. Quick round — not a presentation. Listen for:
- Did they use Best Bet / Street Cred to find strong pieces?
- Did they think about contrast (tempo, style, period)?
- Did they consider the ensemble's level honestly?
- Did they check CBA PML or ASTA relevance?

## Part IV — From Database to Program — 5 min

### Connecting Research to the Deliverable

The Wednesday submission asks you to produce a complete adjudicated repertoire list. Here's what that looks like:

**Your submission should include:**

1. **Ensemble description** — What ensemble are you programming for? (Type, grade level, size, school context)
2. **Festival/event target** — Which assessment? (CBA Concert Band Festival, Colorado ASTA Large Group Festival, etc.)
3. **Program listing** — For each piece:
   - Title, composer, arranger (if applicable)
   - Grade level
   - Approximate duration
   - Best Bet score and Street Cred score from the database
   - One sentence: why this piece for this program
4. **Program rationale** — 2–3 sentences explaining how the pieces contrast and what the program teaches as a whole
5. **Diversity note** — At least one piece by an underrepresented composer (cite ICD data from the database)

### Quick Tips

- Don't just sort by Best Bet and grab the top three. That gives you three "safe" picks but possibly zero contrast.
- Grade level matters. A Grade 6 piece on a Grade 3 program tells the adjudicator you don't know your ensemble.
- Check the `On CBA PML` column if you're programming for a CBA event.
- For orchestra: check the `Ensemble` column — String vs. Full matters.
- Use `Categories` and `Style Tags` to ensure you're not accidentally programming three lyrical slow pieces.

---

## Part V — Closing — 2 min

### The Takeaway

These databases don't replace your ear or your judgment. They're a starting point — a way to see what's been vetted by the profession and how it's held up under real adjudication conditions. The art is in the curation: choosing pieces that work together, that teach something, that challenge your students without breaking them, and that represent the breadth of the repertoire.
